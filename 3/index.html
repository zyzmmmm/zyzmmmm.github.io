Part 2. Computing the "Mid-way Face"

In this task, I need to simultaneously transform both shape and pixels to generate the average face of two images. The parameter n is set as the blending ratio between the two images. For the shape transformation, I used the points selected during the 'defining correspondences' step and calculated the intermediate points of the two images in the (1-n): n ratio. Then, I used the Delaunay function to generate triangles. By solving matrix equations, I obtained the transformation matrix between the triangles. I applied this matrix to transform the pixels of each triangle, and finally, I stitched the triangles together to form the complete image. For the pixel transformation, I blended the pixel values of the two images in the (1-n): n ratio to get the intermediate pixel result.

Part 3. The Morph Sequence

In the previous task, I already obtained the results of blending two facial images at different ratios. We can use warp_frac and dissolve_frac to control the blending of points and pixels, and change them over time to create a video or GIF. Specifically, by adjusting the blending parameters for each frame, we can use the mimsave function from the imageio library to generate a 45-frame GIF, with each frame lasting 1/30 of a second.

Part 4. The "Mean face" of a population

The process of calculating the average face is similar to Part 2, but extends from two images to multiple images. I first calculated the average of all data points, then transformed the corresponding triangles of each image to the average triangle, and averaged the pixels. By selecting a subset of images, such as male or female faces, or smiling faces, we can obtain a specific type of average face.

Part 5. Caricatures: Extrapolating from the mean

By calculating the average face, I can determine the differences between my face and the average face in both shape and pixels. By amplifying these differences, I create a new image where certain features of my face are exaggerated, known as a caricature.
